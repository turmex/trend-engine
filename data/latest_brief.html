LINKEDIN POST DRAFT:
Last week we tackled shoulder pain—the upper body epidemic of desk work. This week, we're dropping down the kinetic chain to the Achilles tendon, which is seeing a spring surge in injuries. Why now? Search interest for Achilles tendonitis just hit 84/100, plantar fasciitis running is up 15% week-over-week, and runner's knee is climbing. The pattern is clear: people are returning to outdoor activity after winter sedentary periods, and the lower leg is paying the price. Here's what most people get wrong about Achilles tendonitis: They rest. They ice. They wait. And the tendon gets weaker. Tendons don't respond to rest the way muscles do. They need LOAD—specifically, eccentric load. That's the lengthening phase of contraction, like lowering your heel slowly off a step. Research consistently shows eccentric heel drops are the most effective non-surgical intervention for Achilles tendinopathy. But here's the nuance from 15,000+ clinical hours: Desk workers and runners get Achilles issues for the SAME reason—soleus weakness. The soleus is the deeper calf muscle that works when your knee is bent. If you sit all day, it atrophies. If you run without training it specifically, it can't handle the load. The gastrocnemius (straight-leg calf muscle) compensates, the Achilles takes excessive strain, and you end up limping. Monday's video covered the 4-exercise protocol: eccentric heel drops, bent-knee calf raises, ankle alphabet, and soleus stretch. It's not woo-woo; it's nervous system regulation that allows tissue remodeling. If you're dealing with Achilles pain right now: 1. Test your ankle dorsiflexion (knee-over-toes without heel lift) 2.
